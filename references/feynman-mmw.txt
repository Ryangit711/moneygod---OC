Feynman Breakdown: Modern Money Mechanics (FRB Chicago, 1992 rev.)
====================================================================

The Central Trick: Banks Create Money When They Lend
------------------------------------------------------
Most people think banks take deposits (money already existing) and lend them out.
Wrong. When a bank makes a loan, it creates a new deposit in the borrower's account.
That deposit didn't exist before. The bank just wrote a number on a ledger.
New money, created from nothing. The loan is an asset. The deposit is a liability.
Both appear on the balance sheet simultaneously. Money is a double-entry invention.

What Constrains This?
---------------------
Not deposits. Banks don't need depositors' money to lend.
What constrains it: RESERVES. Each bank must hold a fraction of its deposits
as reserves (vault cash + balance at the Fed). When a borrower spends the loan,
the money moves to another bank. The lending bank loses reserves to the receiving bank.
If the lending bank doesn't have enough reserves after that drain, it must borrow
reserves from other banks (fed funds market) or from the Fed (discount window).

This is the key chain: Lending creates deposits → deposits move between banks →
reserves shift → the system as a whole is constrained by TOTAL reserves,
which only the Fed controls.

The Fed Controls the Total
---------------------------
The Fed adds reserves by buying government securities from banks (pays by crediting
their reserve accounts). It drains reserves by selling securities. This is
OPEN MARKET OPERATIONS — the primary policy tool. Reserve requirements are secondary;
they set the minimum, but the Fed targets the FED FUNDS RATE (the price of reserves),
and supplies whatever quantity is needed to hit that rate.

Other factors also affect reserves:
- Treasury payments (TT&L accounts — when Treasury spends, reserves increase;
  when Treasury collects taxes, reserves drain)
- Currency in circulation (when people withdraw cash, reserves leave the banking system)
- Foreign exchange intervention (sterilized by the Fed to neutralize impact on reserves)
- Float (checks in process — temporarily inflates reserves)
- Foreign deposits at the Fed

The Fed offsets all these to keep total reserves at the desired level.

The Multiplier Isn't Fixed
---------------------------
Textbook says: with 10% reserve requirement, $1 of reserves supports $10 of deposits.
Reality: the multiplier varies because:
1. Banks hold excess reserves (don't lend every dollar)
2. Currency drains (people hold cash, which doesn't multiply)
3. Different institutions have different reserve requirements
4. Not all deposits are in the money supply (interbank deposits, govt deposits)
5. Banks lend based on credit demand, not reserve arithmetic

The 1992 revision notes that contemporaneous reserve accounting (1984) and
the Monetary Control Act (1980) made the multiplier more stable than the 1970s.
But the relationship between reserves and money is still not fixed.
